How Bridging Loans Work

A bridging loan is a short-term facility designed to cover the period between buying a new property and selling your existing home. By leveraging equity in your current residence, you unlock funds for your new purchase, with the loan typically repaid once your original property sells.

Key Features:

  • Loan terms typically range from 6 to 12 months, matched to your sale timeline
  • Interest capitalisation: No monthly repayments required during the bridging period. Interest is calculated and added to the loan balance, payable at settlement
  • Peak Debt: The maximum loan amount when owning both properties
  • End Debt: Your ongoing loan position following the sale, usually converting to a standard home or investment loan

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a bridging loan and how does it work?
A bridging loan bridges the financial gap between buying and selling properties. It uses your current home's equity, is repaid at settlement, and allows you to act decisively without having to coordinate simultaneous transactions.
How much can I borrow?
Your borrowing power is determined by financial position, property values, and Loan-to-Value Ratio (LVR). We provide comprehensive Peak Debt and End Debt analysis for clarity.
What interest rates apply?
Bridging loans are generally priced higher than standard mortgages due to their short-term nature and risk profile. Our relationships allow us to source competitive rates and potential discounts.
Should I buy or sell first—and how does a bridging loan help?
A bridging loan offers certainty and flexibility: purchase your next home first, secure negotiating power, and avoid temporary accommodation. Risk appetite and market conditions are key factors.
How do repayments work?
Most bridging facilities offer interest capitalisation—no regular repayments are required until sale proceeds settle the loan. Accrued interest is paid out at settlement, reducing cash flow strain during transition.
